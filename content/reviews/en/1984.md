This is not a novel about the future—it's about **the present you think you're living in**. Everyone should read *1984*, but no one should read it merely as fiction. It's more like a message sent from ruins, an iron plaque nailed to the wall of memory, inscribed: "Don't say you weren't warned."

## The Graveyard of Thought is Made of Adjectives

The most terrifying thing about *1984* isn't "Big Brother is watching you," nor Room 101, but the concept of "Newspeak": a language design that can turn lies into common sense and fear into justice. Language is no longer used to express thought, but to shut it down.

That's not fiction. You've already seen it in today's daily life.

When the government says "harmony and stability," do you feel a shadow of "war is peace"?  
When the media says "this is for your own good," do you hear the echo of "ignorance is strength"?

Orwell understood the violence of adjectives. He wrote this book to ensure that after reading it, you would never again easily trust words like "moderate," "positive," or "manageable."

## Love Dies in the Electric Chair

In the latter half of *1984*, Orwell cleanly destroys everything a literary novel should have: love, hope, revolution, loyalty. This book refuses all the climax and redemption readers expect; it's like an ice wall that only gives echoes to those who strike it.

> "He loved Big Brother."

These five words at the end are enough to silence anyone for a long time. Not because of the twist, but because this is language's final breath, the closing bell for humans as thinking animals.

## It Deserves 10 Points, Not Because It's "Perfect"

*1984* was never a perfect book. The narrative is sometimes deliberately flat, characters are paper-thin, metaphors too direct. But we're not here looking for literary technique—we're here looking for **the sound of prophecy being fulfilled**.

It deserves 10 points not because of its literary craftsmanship, but because it told us—

> **When language is controlled, reality disappears.**

And now, what controls language isn't the Big Brother in the novel, but the apps you open every day, the words you choose for "convenience," the slogans you never challenge.

Do you still dare say you haven't read *1984*?  
Are you sure it's not reality yet?
