Circe succeeds where many mythological retellings fail—it creates a psychologically complex character rather than simply updating ancient stories with modern attitudes. Miller's prose carries genuine lyrical beauty, and her reimagining of Circe as a figure of growing power and self-determination feels both fresh and earned. Yet the novel occasionally betrays its contemporary origins in ways that dilute its mythic force.

## The Recovery of Female Agency

Miller excels at excavating agency from a character traditionally defined by her effect on male heroes. Circe's transformation from powerless nymph to confident witch unfolds with psychological realism that grounds the fantastical elements. The novel's greatest strength lies in making this ancient figure feel genuinely present rather than merely symbolic.

## Prose That Earns Its Beauty

Unlike many contemporary fantasy novels, Miller's language justifies its poetic ambitions. Her descriptions of divine power, natural magic, and immortal loneliness achieve genuine sublimity without feeling overwrought. The voice feels appropriately mythic—elevated without being pretentious.

## The Contemporary Intrusion

The novel's weakness emerges when modern psychological concepts overwhelm ancient mythic logic. Circe's relationships occasionally feel more like therapy sessions than divine encounters. Her empowerment narrative, while compelling, sometimes reads more like contemporary self-help than ancient wisdom.

## Mythology as Metaphor vs. Reality

Miller treats the mythological elements primarily as metaphors for human experience rather than engaging with their otherworldly strangeness. While this makes the story accessible, it also domesticates the genuinely alien aspects of ancient Greek religion and cosmology.

7.8 recognizes Miller's considerable achievement in creating literature rather than mere genre fiction, while acknowledging the tension between contemporary feminist values and ancient mythic frameworks.
