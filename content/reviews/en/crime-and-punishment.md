Crime and Punishment stands as one of the monuments of psychological realism, lauded for its unflinching portrayal of guilt, moral reasoning, and spiritual redemption. Raskolnikov's torment is hailed as one of literature's most penetrating studies of conscience. Yet beneath the celebrated psychology lies a novel that ultimately retreats from its own radical premises into the comfort of orthodox Christianity.

## The Genius of Psychological Torment

Dostoevsky's dissection of Raskolnikov's mind remains unmatched in its intensity and precision. The fever dreams, the paranoia, the oscillation between grandiosity and self-loathing—all rendered with a clinical accuracy that predates Freud by decades. The murder itself is secondary; the real drama unfolds in the corridors of consciousness where guilt breeds and multiplies.

## The Retreat into Orthodoxy

Yet the novel's greatest weakness is its need to resolve what should remain unresolvable. Raskolnikov's "extraordinary man" theory—the idea that certain individuals transcend conventional morality—deserves serious philosophical engagement. Instead, Dostoevsky reduces it to mere pride and delusion, dismissible through Christian love. The epilogue's Siberian redemption feels less like organic development than authorial intervention.

## Woman as Salvation

Sonia exists primarily as a vessel for Christian mercy, her own subjectivity subordinated to her redemptive function. Her suffering prostitution becomes sanctified backdrop for male spiritual crisis. Even Dunya, more assertive than most Dostoevskian women, serves mainly to illuminate male moral positions. The novel's women carry the burden of salvation while being denied full interiority.

## The Evasion of Social Critique

For all its psychological penetration, the novel sidesteps systemic analysis. Poverty, social inequality, the conditions that produce both crime and desperation—these remain background texture rather than structural critique. Raskolnikov's crime emerges from philosophical hubris rather than social circumstance, deflecting attention from material conditions toward individual moral failing.

7.2 acknowledges the technical mastery and psychological insight while recognizing how the novel ultimately domesticates its own dangerous ideas. It remains a powerful exploration of guilt—and a monument to literature's tendency to comfort rather than confront.
