This book attempts to weave a feminine chorus from disaster's ashes, but what remains are isolated monologues that neither collide nor merge. The women are placed in separate narrative structures, each carrying her own heartbreak history, yet none truly generates a theory of pain.

Adichie's language remains clean and precise, occasionally cutting to the heart. But this cutting resembles cosmetic surgery rather than dissection: beautiful knife work that never reaches the bone marrow. The sharpness that once intertwined thought and narrative in her earlier works has, in *Dream Count*, devolved into repetitive gestures and postural displays.

This isn't a problem of language, but a misplacement of the novel as form. Adichie seems to no longer believe that friction between characters can generate thought; she merely allows characters to become vessels for her own viewpoints. Her narrative doesn't question complexity but chooses to prove. Each voice advocates for a certain position rather than wavering in dialectic.

This novelistic vision is the literary illusion of the TED generation: believing language can save faith, while forgetting that literature's true power comes from the echoes of faith's collapse.

*Dream Count* does attempt to question how patriarchy, colonialism, and Americanism fracture female experience. But these issues become over-semanticized, losing the novel's necessary ambiguity and contradiction, transforming into a simulation of plot under ideological preset. It's not that the text lacks position, but that it treats position as destination rather than starting point.

This isn't Adichie's worst work, but it's certainly her most "safe." It says the right things, uses the right language, published in the right era, yet leaves no proper wound in literature.

**What she counts in dreams isn't dreams, but readers' sympathy and resonance. And we've grown weary of being invited into rallying novels.**

This isn't a counting of dreams,
but a memory loss of thought.
