In this astonishing novel, Susan Choi doesn't describe a childhood—she dismantles how a childhood gets reconstructed by memory, captured by language, and yet remains ultimately unspeakable. The protagonist Louisa's psychological landscape becomes the battlefield of the entire book, where language serves as the sole witness, weapon, and remains.

The title "Flashlight" is both object and symbol: it illuminates not truth but emptiness; not remembered fragments but the gaps of forgetting. The novel's first section (nearly a standalone novella) opens and closes with the flashlight, forming a spiritual noose: the father's disappearance, the mother's pathological presence, the psychologist and society's attempts to "label" Louisa—everything resembles the flashlight's beam, revealing only fragments while giving darkness more definite form.

This novel refuses narrative inertia. Louisa's perspective shifts between knife-sharp clarity and nightmarish distortion. Her refusal and silence constitute rebellion against the entire adult world—not explosive anger, but precisely designed cold violence. She steals not from lack but as a challenge to meaning; she lies not to escape truth but because she understands that "truth" has long since failed.

The book's passages often reject dramatic climax. For instance, when the psychologist attempts to name Louisa's behavior with psychological terminology like "shock," Louisa responds with children's science—reducing "shock" to the physical effects of balloon friction and electric contact. This isn't misunderstanding but deconstruction; she knows what he means but refuses to give him linguistic passage.

The novel's second half unfolds another narrative about a boy named Seok growing up in the liminal space between colonial and postwar Korea and Japan, where identity and linguistic transformation become core conflicts. This seems separate from Louisa's section but actually resonates on levels of language and history: both characters deal with memory's "displacement," language's "colonization," and identity's "simulacra."

Susan Choi offers no narrative consolation in this work. She makes language repeat, fold, overlap, and flash back continuously—readers must construct their own maps, decide their own boundaries between light and shadow. This isn't a novel to "understand" but a reading experience to "endure."

9.0 out of 10, because it doesn't try to heal you but forces you to see how trauma's language forms its own system.
