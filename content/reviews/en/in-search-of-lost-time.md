*In Search of Lost Time* has been deified as a modernist epic, but when you truly examine it, you find this is not an epic but a performance of self-contemplation, a prolonged echo of time and memory.

## The Religious Ritual of Memory

The narrator revolves around "involuntary memory" triggered by tea and madeleine cakes, with the Madeleine event becoming its religious ritual. The entire book resembles a meditation record of memory rather than authentic emotion. After the epiphany in *Time Regained*, the whole book is celebrated as a paradigm of "writing as translating impressions into spiritual equivalents," but this is more Proust's ritualistic aesthetic of the self rather than truly opening doors to the external world.

Proust's language is pleasing yet heavy, each long sentence like a protracted musical movement that barely allows the reader to breathe. He deconstructs time, love, dreams, and society, but these elements ultimately extend themselves within internal mirrors. He doesn't let us see the world, but makes us stare at our own gaze, gradually losing our way out.

## The Memory Machine and Puppet Theater

He created a memory machine, weaving all characters and events into jungle details and psychological measurements. Deleuze once pointed out: Proust portrays himself as a "universal schizophrenic," with Charlus and Albertine like puppets in his mental network—this artistic insight emphasizes language constructing itself rather than the sting of external reality.

Length is both the symphony and shackle of this book: over four million words, two thousand characters, seven volumes, seemingly presupposing that any reader should worship patience and detail. But in other words, it's a literary endurance test rather than a perceptual shock. It calls itself a revolutionary novel yet remains deeply mired in voyeurism and narration of Parisian aristocratic life, always alienated from the lower classes, the non-belonging, and the "heterogeneity" of experience.

## Excessive Narcissism and Linguistic Stagnation

Certainly, this work has undeniable status in academia and literary history. Its linguistic experiments are far-reaching, making significant contributions to novel form and stream-of-consciousness narrative; it also achieves a kind of consciousness-splitting reconciliation between memory and art, becoming a tool for numerous analyses and recreations by later generations.

But for me, its greatest problem lies not in its flaws but in its excess: excessive narcissism, excessive language, excessive memory-centrism. This is not true insight but a kind of self-repeating cerebral respiration; not text cutting into life, but thought stagnating after being captured by language. If you crave narrative tension, the texture of social reality, or the vitality of characters, this book will only be an extended silence.

I give it 5.4 points: respect for its ambition and linguistic power, but also rejection of its mechanical and hysterical self-obsession. It may be a mountain of modern fiction, but it's also a forest that leaves one breathless.
