R. F. Kuang did not write an adventure but a dissection: how knowledge, in the supposedly noble halls of academia, becomes a form of torture. The eight layers of hell in this novel are not mythological—they are replicas of academic institutions. Logic is turned into an instrument of pain, dissertation into shackles, “education” into cruelty laid bare. Reading *Katabasis* will not remind you of Odysseus or Dante, but of countless graduate students’ exhaustion, anxiety, and suffocated brilliance—the feeling of slowly choking on academic language.

## Language as Torture

Kuang’s novel is saturated with theory and allusion, yet she does not flaunt erudition—she exposes how theory itself becomes a mechanism of oppression. Every dense philosophical citation is a trap: you think you are reading knowledge, but you are undergoing interrogation. This is the novel’s most merciless strategy—it forces you to feel the pain the characters endure, not merely observe it. *The Washington Post* derided it as “bloated,” but that is precisely Kuang’s weapon: the labyrinth of jargon is not a flaw but the very material of hell’s walls.

## Hell Is Academia

The hell in this novel is not metaphysical; it is painfully concrete, intimately familiar. Classrooms, meetings, peer competition, the power of advisors—these academic realities are projected into the layers of fantasy hell. *The Guardian* declared, “Hell is a campus.” This is not just a headline, but the book’s central thesis: academia is not humanity’s heaven of reason, but its purgatory of power and dread. Students are not seekers of knowledge but survivors, perfecting the posture of suffering.

## The Splintered Characters

The characters in *Katabasis* are not likeable. You will not love them, but they will not let you go. This is what *Grimdark Magazine* called the quality of “characters you hate yet cannot abandon.” They are not beacons of humanity but fissures in it. Their anxiety, depression, betrayal, and survival are mirrors forcing readers to admit: in such a hell, no one is clean.

## The Pact Between Literature and Violence

Here, Kuang forges an alliance between language and violence. She does not use story to console the reader, but to wound them. The result is bifurcated: some call it “unreadable,” others find themselves gasping long after finishing. This imbalance is deliberate—it is her ambition. Literature should hurt; otherwise, it is mere entertainment.

## Why we give it a 9.2

*Katabasis* is not perfect. Its language is excessively dense, its pacing fractured, sometimes nearly self-destructive. Yet it is precisely for these reasons that its honesty is undeniable. The novel confronts us with a raw truth: knowledge is never neutral. It enlightens and it torments. And when academia becomes torment, it is indistinguishable from hell.

**9.2/10**, because it strips away any illusion that knowledge can be met only with romance or reverence—it forces us to acknowledge that we have always already been burning in its flames.
