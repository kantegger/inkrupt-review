Reading *The Second Sex* is not merely encountering a monumental work of thought—it's like sitting in the dock, hearing another subject of human history finally speak, revealing how she has been named and constructed as "the Other." Simone de Beauvoir is not writing about "women"; she is dissecting how "becoming woman" as a cultural project has been jointly constructed by history, religion, philosophy, science, and literature into a tyranny disguised as nature.

## The Invention of the Other, and the Subject's Lie

"One is not born, but rather becomes, woman." This sentence has been quoted so many times it has almost become a slogan, but if you have truly read *The Second Sex*, you know this is not just a sentence—it's a gunshot aimed at the core of metaphysics. What Beauvoir dismantles is not gender, but how the subject, under the guise of "universality," establishes itself upon the Other. Women are merely the most obvious victims; in fact, all beings labeled as "different" are raw material for this narrative machine.

## Existentialism: Not Personal Liberation, but Historical Responsibility

Beauvoir does not triumph through emotion or anger; she uses philosophical backbone to strike at modernity. Her weapons are Heidegger, Sartre, Hegel, Freud—summoning them one by one only to knock them back into shape. She uses existentialism as a blade to cut open the hypocrisy of the word "freedom" in gender reality. Becoming a woman is not an option, but a process of being deprived of options.

There are no inspirational quotes here, no healing tone—only cold, massive, almost cruel dialectics. She forces every reader—regardless of gender—to rethink where your so-called "nature," "essence," "love," "family," and "roles" come from, and whom they serve.

## Literature and Body: Organs Betrayed by Language

Particularly noteworthy is her critique of literature: Beauvoir is not anti-literature, but opposes those works that continue to reproduce female symbols in the name of literature and aesthetics. She names Balzac, Maupassant, even Zola, cutting open how the women in their writing are always de-agentized, desired, and romantically disappeared into the background of male narratives.

She also writes about the cultural situation of the female body, not packaged in today's popular body positivity, but directly confronting the mechanisms of humiliation, pathologization, consumption, and control that make the body a battlefield for sovereignty.

## Why Not 10 Points?

Because *The Second Sex* is evidence of a writer's excessive force—it is too massive, too comprehensive, too eager to write about all problems at once. It inevitably has omissions (the absence of transgender and non-binary gender considerations) and cannot escape the limitations of 20th-century philosophical vocabulary. But precisely because of this, it appears even more astonishing: one person, one pen, could approach truth of such scale.

9.8 is a tribute to its intellectual scale and linguistic violence, and also a reminder: revolution should not be written too smoothly.

This is not a book that makes you "become a better version of yourself," but one that makes you unable to believe that the question "who are you" can exist independently.
