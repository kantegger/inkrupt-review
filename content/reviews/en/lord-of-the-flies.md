Golding commits intellectual corner-cutting here. Place British public school boys on desert island, watch them rapidly devolve into savagery—this premise isn't insight but confirmation-bias apparatus. Golding wants to prove "human nature is evil," designs artificial environment where all civilizational buffers (law, family, historical memory) are systematically removed, then feigns surprise: see, human nature is indeed evil.
This fraudulence of experimental design is the book's fatal flaw. Real civilizational collapse (Holocaust, genocide) never occurs in vacuum; it happens in most developed civilizations, executed by best-educated people. Golding's island is safe theater where readers watch violence without feeling accused—because boys get rescued, because it's "primitive state," because we tell ourselves: this isn't us, this is stripped-down human nature.
But this is Golding's class and race complicity. Why British public school boys? Because they represent "civilization's highest achievement," their fall therefore more "universal." This elitist narcissism—only our fall counts—exposes Golding's perspective: non-Western "savagery" unworthy of writing, never "civilized"; working-class violence unworthy, never "cultured." Only Eton boys killing on islands is philosophy.
"Lord of the Flies" (flies in rotting pig's head) as symbol is theological laziness. Simon's epilepsy, mystical dialogue, mistaken killing—this plotline blames violence on "misunderstanding" and "fear," not structural power competition. Jack's pig-hunting obsession isn't fascism's germ but adolescent male testosterone; Piggy's glasses aren't knowledge's fragility but myopia's metaphoric burden. Golding psychologizes social conflict, avoiding political analysis.
Most hypocritical is ending: naval officer appears, children cry, "weeping for the end of innocence and darkness of man's heart." This scene is double absolution—officer represents civilizational violence's highest form (from warship), yet judges children's violence; readers' moral superiority restored because we "recognized" savagery. But recognized what? Merely false civilization/savagery dichotomy—we civilized, so not savage; they savage because not in civilization.
4.8/10. Golding's sentences are taut, island description has sensory immediacy, Ralph-Jack confrontation has dramatic tension. But these technical virtues serve intellectually bankrupt premise: human nature testable in isolation, evil geographically locatable (put on island), civilization reducible to rules not ongoing social struggle.
Reading this, I return to your library's 1984. Orwell shows savagery within civilization—Ministry of Love, Ministry of Truth, Room 101—systemic violence by suited men. Golding outsources savagery to island, keeping civilization innocent. This isn't courage but evasion; not insight but placebo.
