Vivek Shanbhag proves once again that he isn't writing stories—he's constructing moral labyrinths. *Sakina's Kiss* opens with a linguistic trap: a mundane domestic scene, a seemingly harmless visit, two boys with peculiar smiles, and suddenly, the novel shifts from family drama into psychological thriller. This is a rare maneuver in South Asian fiction: making linguistic restraint a form of violence, burying suspicion and fear in the crevices of middle-class speech.

The protagonist, Venkat, is the archetype of the Indian middle-class father—rational, gentle, progressive on the surface, yet riddled with tension, suspicion, and an inability to navigate shifting gender and generational dynamics. His language alternates between mechanisms of self-justification and poor translations of inner confusion. His exchanges with his wife, Viji, are the novel’s most precise moments: they share a middle-class linguistic world built from self-help book vocabulary—comforting, sarcastic, and emotionally distanced.

The tension lies in the weaponization of uncertainty. Like the protagonist, the reader is never sure if MP3 and RK are foolish boys in love or latent threats; whether daughter Rekha is an agent of free choice or a projection of parental anxiety. The novel teeters on the boundary between intelligence and misreading; every narrative feels like a defensive version, not crafted to reflect truth, but to control its framing.

This is Shanbhag’s most chilling skill—not making characters lie, but making them believe their words are already truth. This isn’t unreliable narration; this is the terrifying everyday discourse: calm, rational, flawless on the surface, yet silently excluding dissent, sanitizing emotion, and rationalizing control.

*Sakina’s Kiss* isn’t remarkable for its plot twists, but for its surgical insight into language and class. It shows us that language isn’t a medium of communication, but an architecture of social order; not a tool for opinion, but a firewall of middle-class self-preservation.

The novel ends without moral judgment. You’re left trapped in its linguistic cul-de-sac, replaying the phrase “just came to discuss the project” and tasting its embedded power. This isn’t a suspense novel—but it's more unsettling than any murder mystery.

8.7, for its restraint, its quiet, and the cursed kiss it plants inside the language itself.
