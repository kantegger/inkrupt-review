*The Little Prince* should not be read as a harmless children's book, nor does it deserve to invoke that phrase "what is essential is invisible to the eye" as self-hypnotic consolation.

Written during the author's New York exile from 1942-43, the book was initially published in English and French in 1943, only appearing in mainland France after the post-war liberation in 1945/46. Though it has since been translated into over 600 languages, becoming a translation-level classic second only to the Bible, its simplified allegorical structure and meaning have gradually transformed from philosophical thought into market symbols, like an expanding semantic void.

The text claims to reveal cognitive differences between adults and children, but this is merely a projection of the author's emotional repression and resentment toward escaping adulthood. The adult world is lightly sketched as a framework of rational suppression and non-imaginative containers, with the narrator seemingly believing that "grown-ups never understand anything" suffices. This starting point is inevitably shallow, instead revealing the author's fear of "maturity" and nostalgia.

From a translation perspective, the first English translator Katherine Woods, to accommodate the redemptive zeitgeist of the 1940s, rendered Saint-Exupéry's "serious things" as "matters of consequence," adding moral coloring and strengthening allegorical deconstructive energy. However, this morally-driven translation compressed the original text's ambiguity and fluidity. *The Little Prince*'s charm lies precisely in the language's ambiguity and irony; Woods' operation imperceptibly pushed it toward didactic edges.

I do not deny this book's cultural status, but this simplified and over-polished atmospheric philosophy serves more as a supplement for adults seeking childhood emotional suturing rather than a philosophical text that truly changes thinking structures. Some readers even argue it represents adults' idealized projection onto children—a romanticized reconstruction of childhood memories rather than genuine naive expression.

In conclusion, *The Little Prince* is neither an answer nor a philosophical feast, but a fable corrupted by market healing mechanisms. Its language is ornately decorated yet may not necessarily bear depth. I give it 4.2 points—both for the possibility it once had to peel away cultural shells and for the reality of its ultimate commercialization and emotional commodification.
