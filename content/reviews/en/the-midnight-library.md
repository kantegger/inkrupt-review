The premise of *The Midnight Library* isn't actually bad—a dying woman explores countless parallel lives in a library, trying to find the path of "what if I had chosen differently." The concept feels like *Sliding Doors* meets *It's a Wonderful Life*, with a dash of *Rick and Morty*'s multiverse spice. Sounds enticing, doesn't it?

Unfortunately, this book just keeps repeating one message: "Living itself is good, you need to learn to love yourself."

## Cheap Positivity

The problem with this book isn't that it's "positive," but that its "positivity" is too flat and cheap. Almost every chapter is a simplified parable where the protagonist enters a version of herself, discovers that path isn't perfect either, then learns a little lesson. These "lessons" look like short quotes from a life coach's Instagram—shallow, sweet, empty.

> "You don't have to understand life. You just have to live it."
> "Never underestimate the big importance of small things."
> "You are enough."

This isn't a novel, this is copy.

## Pandering Language

The language level is even more disappointing. Matt Haig's writing rhythm and syntax seem designed to accommodate readers' attention thresholds: short sentences, big fonts, similes, as if afraid readers might forget "this is a touching passage" before swiping to the next page on the subway. This is the novel pandering to readers, not challenging them.

## Pseudo-Existentialism

The most critical issue is this: the book wants to imitate Camus, imitate Bloch, even imitate Murakami, but what it ultimately offers isn't confusion about "being," but rationalization of "being"—it's not existentialism, it's emotional self-help.

I understand why it's a bestseller. I understand why it had a market during the pandemic. But we're not a healing bookstore, we're critics. The problem with this book isn't that it's "light," but that it pretends to be philosophical literature while actually being a TED Talk written in prose.

## Conclusion

If you're just emerging from a life low point, this book might give you some warmth. But if you want to read literature that truly encounters existential pain, then please go read Li Yiyun, Knausgård, or Agota Kristof.

What this book ultimately makes one want to say is:

**"Life certainly has infinite possibilities, but that doesn't mean every one is worth writing into a book."**
