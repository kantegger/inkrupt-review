One day, Yeong-hye decides she will no longer eat meat. That's it. No religious reasons, no health concerns, no moral stance about animal rights. She simply doesn't want to eat meat anymore. This seemingly simple decision becomes, in Han Kang's hands, a bloody war over women's bodily autonomy.

*The Vegetarian* is divided into three parts, telling the story of one woman's breakdown from three different perspectives. The first part is from her husband's viewpoint, the second from her brother-in-law's, and the third from her sister's. Notice: there is no section from Yeong-hye's own perspective. She remains the object of observation, never the subject of narration. This narrative structure itself is an indictment of patriarchal society.

## The Body's Rebellion

Yeong-hye's refusal to eat meat is actually her body's rebellion against long-term repression. In her husband's eyes, this is his wife's disobedience; in her brother-in-law's view, it's a primitive form of sexuality; to her sister, it's a symptom of mental illness. But Han Kang shows us these are all interpretations filtered through the male gaze—the real Yeong-hye remains absent throughout.

The most disturbing passage in the book is the scene where Yeong-hye is force-fed. When she refuses to eat, the men around her decide to use violence to make her "return to normal." This scene is both literal and symbolic—women's bodies never belong to themselves, but to family, society, and state.

> Her lips were sealed shut, as if protecting some secret. But they pried open her mouth and forced her to swallow what she had refused. This wasn't feeding; this was rape.

## The Logic of Madness

Yeong-hye's ultimate "madness"—her belief that she is a tree, her refusal to wear clothes or speak—is not portrayed by Han Kang as pathological, but as a form of extreme rationality. In a world filled with violence against women, perhaps madness is the most reasonable response.

Han Kang's prose is calm and precise. She doesn't sensationalize or preach; she simply observes this disaster with cold eyes. This calmness makes the violence feel more real, more unforgivable.

## A Portrait of East Asian Patriarchy

Though the story takes place in Korea, *The Vegetarian* depicts patriarchal violence that is universal. Yeong-hye's experience reminds us of all those women who are asked to be "normal," to be "obedient," to "sacrifice themselves for the family."

Han Kang won the International Booker Prize in 2016 for this work, becoming the first Korean author to receive this honor. But more important than this recognition is the question the book poses: when a woman says "no," why does the world panic so violently?

*The Vegetarian* is not an easy novel to digest. It sticks in you like a splinter, continuing to ache long after you've finished reading. I give it an 8.7 because it courageously depicts truths we'd rather not face, even when those truths make us uncomfortable.

Some books are written to entertain, some to enlighten, but *The Vegetarian* is written to awaken. It reminds us that in this seemingly civilized world, violence is still everywhere, especially violence against women. And sometimes, the only way to refuse participation in this violent system is to refuse to be a "normal person."
