This is not a book written for readers; it's not even a book. It's more like a record of language fractures forming scabs, an experiment in continuous whispers at the edges of inner wounds.

Yiyun Li doesn't want to tell stories—she wants to dismantle the structure of storytelling itself. If you're reading this book still looking for narrative arc, emotional release, character development, you've walked into the wrong room entirely. Every page here represents language's final breaths before suicide.

Mother and son are no longer characters but personifications of concepts themselves. The son is like language's own shadow, sometimes raising doubts, sometimes creating interference. Not for communication, but to undermine communication's very premise. The mother is not grief's embodiment but language's final residue still insisting on existence.

The title "Things in Nature Merely Grow" is itself an irony against "growth narratives." This book never believes anything develops naturally, never pretends time can heal anything. It knows: language cannot replace loss; it can only repeatedly carve that emptiness.

This is not writing for healing but to prevent silence from completely occupying writing's territory. What Yiyun Li writes down is not longing for her son but a test report on language's ability to maintain minimal tension. Language is like a kite with a broken string—she's not trying to pull it back but to prove: even as it falls, that line still cuts.

This is not a work that makes you cry; it disdains moving you. It wants you to understand: true pain is not being unable to speak it, but speaking it and discovering language fundamentally cannot bear it.

9.3 out of 10, for its minimalism, for its irresolution, for that one cut it still delivers within its coldness.
