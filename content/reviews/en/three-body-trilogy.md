Liu Cixin's *Three-Body* trilogy undoubtedly holds a landmark position in Chinese science fiction literature, but when examined through the lens of language and thought, the work's value and problems are equally immense.

From a linguistic perspective, *The Three-Body Problem* is not oriented toward literariness; its narrative resembles technical reports and conceptual simulations more than literature. The vocabulary is straightforward, emotions mechanical, character development purely functional—this is not a flaw, but a choice, a decision to abandon linguistic tension and retain only conceptual drive. This linguistic structure serves a grander intention: to transform the novel into a vessel for cosmic speculation, to turn narrative into a framework for thought experiments.

But precisely because of this, the world of *Three-Body* is cold. Not austere, but ice-cold, lacking human warmth. Characters like Cheng Xin are not designed to reveal ethical dilemmas, but are used as tools to demonstrate the bankruptcy of humanism. This narrative attitude is undoubtedly radical, using the "Dark Forest Law" to regard all morality as fragile and all beliefs as illusory. Science fiction becomes the spokesperson for philosophical allegory, but this allegory is unidirectional—it is judgment, not dialectic.

*Three-Body*'s ambition lies in establishing a post-anthropocentric cosmology, where language is algorithm, ethics are obstacles, and survival is the only legitimacy. This extreme evolutionary philosophy is unprecedented in the Chinese-speaking world, and thus dazzling. But its danger lies precisely here: it is anti-humanistic, not only in thought but in narrative structure, deconstructing "humanity" into inorganic components. It is not imagination of the future, but skepticism toward human nature.

We cannot deny that this work opened up new horizons for Chinese science fiction, nor can we deny its overwhelming advantage in imagination. But we must also acknowledge that it is a trial of civilization that offers no quarter, an anti-ethical experiment conducted against the backdrop of a cruel universe.

Such a work should neither be easily praised nor simply dismissed. It is a mirror, reflecting our projections about technology, survival, and our own values. Only, that mirror surface is too cold, too hard to reflect humanity—it only shows a lightless cosmic abyss.

7.4 points, for its philosophical violence, and for its literary abandonment.
