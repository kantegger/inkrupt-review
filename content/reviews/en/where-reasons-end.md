Yiyun Li is not writing a novel this time. She is searching for words in the wreckage, trying to build a room where one can converse with death. The subtitle of this book should be: "A mother and her non-existent child speaking at the edge of language." But she didn't add a subtitle, even deliberately erasing the narrative framework, as if any explanation would be disrespectful to the pain.

This is not a book you would find on the "recommended reading" shelf in bookstores, unless that bookstore also sells silence, regret, and the collapse of language. While most people talk about "healing literature," this book's existence is like a cold glance at the entire therapeutic culture: "I don't want to be healed, nor do I need you to understand."

Is this an arbitrary stance? Yes. But it is therefore utterly sincere.

## The Dialectic of Language

Language here is both tool and obstacle. The mother in the book (the author's surrogate) engages in a dialectic with her deceased son Nikolai—one that has only language, no voice. This dialogue is like tuning at a concert—every sentence adjusts frequency, redefines meaning, trying to prevent the word "grief" from falling into language's trap.

For example, this passage:

> "You're being too literary," Nikolai said.  
> "I'm a writer," I replied.  
> "That's not an excuse," he said.

Such sentences are like internal criticism dismantled into dramatic dialogue, where language's powerlessness becomes narrative momentum, then constantly negates and dissolves itself. This dual minimalism of structure and emotion—leaving death in the blank spaces—recalls Susan Sontag's cold gaze and Beckett's desperate humor, yet it's not quite so literary. This is a book that wants to break literature apart and reconstruct it.

## The Power of Honesty

Of course, you could also say this book is "a bit pretentious," "written like an MFA workshop exercise," "has no plot," "emotionally narcissistic"... You'd be right. But in saying these things, you also expose that you're still waiting for a story, while this book has long ceased to belong to stories.

*Where Reasons End* is a book that doesn't want to be summarized, but we still have to give it a rating. I give it 8.9, not because it's perfect, but because it's honest. And honesty, in this era, is an almost obsolete power.
