Jack Gladney is professor of "Hitler studies" who never learned German. This premise is DeLillo's diagnosis: in postmodernity, you can become expert in anything except what actually matters. Jack teaches fascist aesthetics at university, finds peace in supermarket cereal arrangements—White Noise's central paradox: our fear of death is so vast we must constantly drown it in consumption.
Part One is absurd variation on family comedy. Jack, fourth wife Babette, and their children from previous marriages form typical American blended family. They watch TV, shop, discuss Dylar—a mysterious drug supposedly eliminating death fear. DeLillo's dialogue is scalpel: everyone speaks, no one communicates; language fills space like white noise, masking the unspeakable truth—we all die.
"The Airborne Toxic Event" is the book's axis. A derailed train releases chemical cloud; Jack's family evacuates. In this surreal scene, DeLillo shows how technological disaster is mediated—TV coverage more real than cloud itself, evacuation becomes family vacation, and Jack's obsession: exposed to toxin, will he die? Technocrats assure "simulated exposure," but simulation/reality boundaries have collapsed. This is postmodern death: no longer religious, meaningful, but statistical, random, data-managed.
Babette's entanglement with Dylar is the novel's darkest section. To obtain experimental drug, she sleeps with its developer. Jack's response isn't anger but jealousy—she accessed death-fear elimination first. DeLillo shows marriage as conspiracy against death: we unite not for love but to distract from ending. When Jack finally finds and shoots the developer, this is neither revenge nor justice but existential gesture—before death, violence confirms one still lives.
The supermarket is White Noise's church. DeLillo's dizzying long paragraphs describe fluorescent shelves, automatic doors, credit card slides. Here consumption isn't merely economic but existential defense mechanism. When Jack sees "most photographed barn in America," he realizes tourists come not to see barn but to photograph it—media replaces real, representation replaces experience. This is white noise's metaphor: not silence but hum filling silence; not death but daily chatter masking it.
8.9/10. DeLillo occasionally over-intellectualizes (certain passages read like philosophy essays), but reaches rare tenderness in final chapter "Dylar's Supermarket." Jack and daughter's closing supermarket scene is postmodern literature's closest approach to redemption—not through God but through cereal arrangements, cart flows, register sounds. If death is absolute solitude, supermarket is democratic community: everyone buying, everyone dying, everyone pretending unafraid.
Reading this, I keep returning to your library's Klara and the Sun. Ishiguro questions "human heart" through robot's eyes; DeLillo shows through professor's eyes: heart already redefined by TV, supermarkets, pharmaceuticals. If Klara attempts understanding humans through observation, Jack attempts escaping human condition through consumption—both impossible engineering, but DeLillo's version more comic, therefore more sorrowful.
